# HipSci Genome Phasing: Diploid vs Haploid Status

*Research question: when we align Smart-seq2 reads to "donor genomes" from HipSci, are those genomes phased diploid (two haplotypes) or unphased/consensus (one sequence)? What does this mean practically for personalized RNA-seq alignment?*

*Compiled 2026-06-17.*

---

## Summary (TL;DR)

**The publicly released HipSci genotype data is statistically phased.** Variants are called from Illumina HumanCoreExome-12 BeadChip arrays, imputed against a combined 1000 Genomes Phase 1 + UK10K reference panel with IMPUTE2 v2.3.1, then phased with **SHAPEIT v2.r790**. The released per-donor files are VCFs containing pipe-separated genotypes (e.g. `0|1`), i.e. *haplotype-resolved in a statistical sense*. They are NOT trio-phased and NOT long-read phased.

What this means practically:
- You can build a **diploid personalized reference** (two FASTA haplotypes per donor) from the HipSci phased VCF. Tools like `bcftools consensus -H 1` / `-H 2` will materialize haplotype 1 vs haplotype 2 FASTAs.
- The phasing is **locally accurate but globally imperfect**. Modern SHAPEIT v2-class phasing on common SNPs from arrays + imputation has switch-error rates on the order of ~0.5–1% on common variants in unrelated European-ancestry individuals — equivalent to roughly one phasing switch every ~few Mb on average, with much higher error on rare/imputed variants. For *RNA-seq read assignment to a gene*, which only requires phasing to be correct over distances on the order of a single transcript (typically <100 kb), this is more than adequate. For *cross-gene* / *enhancer-promoter* haplotype claims, expect occasional switches.
- The data also includes **WGS on the iPSC lines themselves** (~324 lines @ ~30–42× PCR-free Illumina, deposited on EGA), but the openly released, donor-keyed germline VCF used by downstream papers (Jerber, Cuomo, the HipSci sc-eQTL pipeline) is the array + imputation + SHAPEIT product, not WGS-derived. There is no public release of long-read or trio data for HipSci donors.

For the Arc DNA-LM project: treating each HipSci donor as **two haplotype FASTAs** is the right model. The two haplotypes will typically differ at ~3–4 million sites genome-wide (~1 het variant per ~1 kb), which is enough that reads will mismap to the wrong haplotype if you naively pretend the donor is haploid.

## HipSci Genomics: Phasing Status (what was actually done)

### Source data: arrays, not WGS, for the published germline VCF

From Kilpinen et al. 2017 (*Nature* 546:370 — the flagship HipSci paper) and the methods sections of downstream papers using HipSci (Jerber 2021, Cuomo 2020, Bonder 2024 sc-eQTL update in *HGG Advances*):

- Donor genotyping platform: **Illumina HumanCoreExome-12 v1.0 BeadChip** (sometimes also HumanOmni2.5-8 for a replication subset). ~250–550K directly typed SNPs.
- Imputation: **IMPUTE2 v2.3.1**, reference = combined **UK10K cohorts + 1000 Genomes Phase 1**, in ~5 Mb chunks with 300 kb buffer.
- Phasing: **SHAPEIT v2.r790**, samples phased independently using the reference panel as a haplotype scaffold.
- Output: per-donor single-sample VCF files (merged with `bcftools merge` for downstream uses), genotypes encoded with `|` (phased).

This pipeline is the standard "array + imputation + statistical phasing" stack circa 2015–2017 — same family of methods as 1000 Genomes Phase 3 and HRC. It is *not* family-based / trio phasing; it is *not* read-based phasing.

### Is there donor WGS available?

Yes, but with caveats:
- **iPSC line WGS exists**: HipSci deep-sequenced ~324 fibroblast-derived iPSC lines at ~41× PCR-free Illumina coverage. These were used in the Jakubek et al. 2020 *Nature Communications* HipSci+iPSCORE structural-variant paper (719 WGS samples total, 477 unique donors, ~42× mean coverage). Available via EGA (controlled access; main study EGAS00001001465 + dependent dataset accessions).
- **"Donor germline" vs "iPSC line" distinction**: The WGS is performed on the iPSC line, which has the donor's germline genotype plus accumulated reprogramming/passage mutations and occasional large CNVs. For our purposes (training a DNA LM on the donor genotype) we can treat WGS calls on the iPSC line as a noisy proxy for donor germline; somatic/iPSC-specific variants are individually rare (typically <100s of SNV differences per line vs the donor genome) and are mostly filtered by standard germline calling pipelines.
- **What is publicly released**: HipSci's openly accessible per-line genotype VCFs (downloadable from hipsci.org under "Open access" tier) are the array+imputation+SHAPEIT product. The deep WGS BAMs/VCFs are behind EGA controlled access (managed access agreement required).
- **Some sources state "donor WGS is not available"** (e.g., Schwartzentruber et al. 2018 commentary, and at least one 2019 Stem Cell Reports paper) — meaning the *donor primary tissue* was not WGS'd; only the iPSC line was. This is the correct nuance: donor blood/skin was genotyped on arrays; the derived iPSC was WGS'd.

### Phasing method/resolution

- **Type**: statistical / population reference panel-based phasing (SHAPEIT v2).
- **Not used**: no trio (parents not sequenced), no long reads (no published HipSci PacBio/ONT), no Hi-C, no linked reads.
- **Resolution**: SHAPEIT v2 on common SNPs from arrays + a deep reference panel produces switch errors on the order of:
  - ~0.5–1% per heterozygous site for common SNPs (MAF > 5%) — i.e., a switch every ~100–200 heterozygous sites, which corresponds to roughly every **1–3 Mb** in a typical European-ancestry genome (where het rate is ~1 in 1000–2000 bp at common sites).
  - Substantially worse for rare and imputed variants — switch error can climb to several percent for MAF < 1%.
  - The Delaneau et al. 2019 SHAPEIT4 benchmark and the Loh et al. 2016 Eagle benchmarks (against the HRC panel, more powerful than UK10K+1KG-P1) give the modern lower bound; HipSci's older SHAPEIT v2 + smaller reference panel sits a bit above that.
- **Practical implication**: a SHAPEIT-phased VCF is essentially correctly phased *locally* (within tens of kb to ~1 Mb), but the global "haplotype 1" label may switch identity several times per chromosome.

## What "Diploid Reference Genome" Means in Practice

There are three things people mean by "personalized reference":

1. **Single consensus FASTA with the donor's homozygous variants substituted in, and heterozygous sites left as the reference base** (or as the major allele). This is a *haploid* representation and discards heterozygous information entirely. Cheap and easy, but throws away half the genotype info.

2. **N-masked / IUPAC-masked reference**: a single FASTA where homozygous variant sites get the alternate base and heterozygous sites get either an `N` or a IUPAC ambiguity code. Used by some allele-specific expression pipelines (e.g., older WASP workflows) to avoid mapping bias without committing to a phase. Still effectively haploid; phasing information is discarded.

3. **Diploid personalized reference (two FASTAs)**: separate FASTAs for haplotype 1 ("maternal") and haplotype 2 ("paternal"). Built by applying phased variants to the reference, e.g. `bcftools consensus -H 1 -f GRCh38.fa donor.phased.vcf.gz` and `bcftools consensus -H 2 ...`. Reads are then aligned against the concatenated 2N reference, and best alignment wins. This is the form recommended for allele-specific expression and is the *only* form that preserves phasing info.

For HipSci data, option 3 is what's actionable. The phased VCF gives you genotypes like `0|1` for each het site, where the order `0|1` vs `1|0` matters: putting all the "left" alleles into haplotype A and all the "right" into haplotype B yields a self-consistent diploid pair *to the extent that SHAPEIT got the phasing right*.

Note: "maternal" / "paternal" labels are **not meaningful for HipSci** — without parental sequencing, you don't know which haplotype came from which parent. The labels are just "haplotype A" and "haplotype B", arbitrarily assigned per-chromosome.

## Heterozygosity Rate — Why You Can't Just Use One Sequence

In a typical European-ancestry human genome (HipSci is predominantly UK European):
- ~3.5–4.5 million SNVs differ from GRCh38 in a single donor (total: hom + het).
- ~1.5–2.5 million of those are **heterozygous**.
- This is ~1 het variant per ~1,200–2,000 bp on average — i.e., a Smart-seq2 read of 75–150 bp covering a transcript will frequently overlap a het site.
- Plus ~500K–800K short indels, ~3,000 SVs, ~50,000 mobile element insertions — though the array+imputation+SHAPEIT pipeline only captures SNPs and short indels in the reference panel.

**Why this matters for RNA-seq alignment**:
- If you align to GRCh38 directly: the donor's variants create persistent mismatches at every het site, biasing alignment quality scores and over-rejecting reads carrying the alt allele (classical "reference bias").
- If you align to a single consensus (haploid) personalized reference with major-allele substituted at het sites: you arbitrarily resolve het sites in favor of whichever allele you picked, again biasing read counts at those sites.
- If you align to a diploid personalized reference (two FASTAs, best alignment wins): reads carrying either allele will find a perfect match on the appropriate haplotype, eliminating reference bias.

For our DNA-LM training objective (model predicts RNA coverage from sequence): we need the *sequence we feed the model* to match the *sequence the reads were transcribed from*. Het sites that are mis-resolved on the input side directly translate to noise in the training target.

## HipSci publications discussing phasing

- **Kilpinen et al. 2017** (*Nature* 546:370): Methods explicitly state SHAPEIT v2.r790 phasing after IMPUTE2 imputation, with UK10K+1000G Phase 1 reference panel. This is the canonical citation for the HipSci phasing pipeline.
- **Bonder et al. 2024** (*HGG Advances*, "Mapping dynamic regulation…"): re-uses the same HipSci phased VCFs (downloaded from hipsci.org) for sc-eQTL mapping; methods reference Kilpinen 2017 for genotype QC.
- **Jakubek et al. 2020** (*Nat Commun* 11:2928): performs WGS-based structural-variant calling on HipSci+iPSCORE iPSC lines but does *not* re-phase; HipSci-specific phasing is inherited from the Kilpinen pipeline.
- **Jerber et al. 2021** (*Nat Genet* 53:304): uses HipSci genotype data for demuxlet donor demultiplexing and for sc-eQTL; cites HipSci data portal and Kilpinen 2017 for genotype processing. Does not re-phase.
- **Cuomo et al. 2020** (*Nat Commun* 11:810): paired Smart-seq2 + WGS dataset (E-MTAB / EGA, see corrected accessions in `4_smartseq_datasets_with_genotype_variation.md`). Uses the HipSci phased VCFs for sc-eQTL and donor demultiplexing.

None of these publications mention long-read or pedigree-based phasing for HipSci donors. The phasing status has been stable since 2017.

## Practical Implications for Our RNA-seq Alignment Pipeline

### What we have to work with
- One **phased VCF per donor** from HipSci (statistical phasing, SHAPEIT v2 on imputed array genotypes).
- Optionally, **deep WGS BAMs/VCFs for the iPSC lines** themselves via EGA controlled access. These would let us call rare/private variants that the array+imputation pipeline misses, but adding them requires:
  - DAC application + managed access agreement.
  - Re-running our own phasing (since the WGS VCFs are unphased by default).
  - For rare variants in particular, the WGS-derived VCF will *not* be well-phased without a reference panel call against a larger cohort.

### Recommended pipeline for diploid personalized alignment
1. Pull the HipSci phased VCF per donor (open access tier, hipsci.org → Files → Genotyping).
2. Build two FASTAs per donor:
   ```
   bcftools consensus -H 1 -f GRCh38.fa donor.phased.vcf.gz > donor.hap1.fa
   bcftools consensus -H 2 -f GRCh38.fa donor.phased.vcf.gz > donor.hap2.fa
   ```
3. Concatenate the two haplotype FASTAs (with chromosome names suffixed `_h1` / `_h2`) into a single 2N reference for the donor.
4. Build STAR/HISAT2 index on the 2N reference. (STAR's "diploid mode" or just running on the concat reference both work; HISAT2 has explicit diploid graph support via `--haplotype`.)
5. Align Smart-seq2 reads; best-of-two-haplotypes mapping happens automatically.
6. For coverage track generation: collapse h1+h2 coverage back onto GRCh38 coordinates using a liftover (the homozygous portion of the donor genome is identical between h1 and h2; het sites get one count from each haplotype to combine).

### Caveats to flag in our pipeline
- **SHAPEIT switch errors don't matter within a single gene** (typical gene <100 kb < typical phase block ≈ 1 Mb). So within-gene allele-specific coverage is well-defined.
- **Switch errors do matter for cross-gene haplotype linking** — if we want to ask "did these two genes co-express on the same chromosome copy?", a phasing switch between them will scramble the answer. Mitigation: re-phase using the RNA-seq reads themselves (long-range RNA-seq phasing tools like phASER), or accept that long-range haplotype claims are noisy.
- **Variants the array+imputation pipeline misses are silently absent from the personalized reference.** Rare donor-private variants (MAF < 0.5% or so) will show up as mismatches against GRCh38 even in the personalized reference. For most reads this is rare enough to ignore; for genes with strong private variation it may matter. Using the iPSC WGS VCF (controlled access) would close this gap.
- **No structural variants in the public VCF.** Large deletions, duplications, inversions are not captured by array+imputation. If a donor has a private SV affecting a transcribed gene, that gene's coverage will be misrepresented. The Jakubek 2020 SV callset (EGA controlled) is the only source for HipSci SVs.

### Quick sanity check we should run
Given a few HipSci donors, compute:
- Number of het sites per donor in the phased VCF (expect 1.5–2.5M).
- Distribution of inferred phase block lengths (expect medians in the Mb range).
- Fraction of Smart-seq2 reads overlapping at least one het site (expect a large majority — most reads on expressed genes will cover at least one).

This validates that the phasing is dense enough to meaningfully separate haplotypes and that the donors do, in fact, have substantial heterozygosity (i.e., they are not the rare highly autozygous individuals where a haploid reference would suffice).
