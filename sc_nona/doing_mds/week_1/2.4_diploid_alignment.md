# Diploid-Aware Alignment of RNA-seq Reads to Personalized Genomes

**Question:** Given Smart-seq2 reads from HipSci iPSC donors and per-donor WGS-based genotypes, what is the best way to align the reads when each donor has a diploid genome? The downstream goal is **base-pair coverage tracks (BigWig)** for a DNA language model, not just gene-level counts or ASE p-values.

---

## TL;DR — Recommended approach (REVISED 2026-06-22)

> **This TL;DR was rewritten after re-deliberation.** The original recommendation was N-masked GRCh37 + STAR+WASP + SNPsplit, on the rationale that cross-donor GRCh37-coordinate BigWigs are convenient for downstream analysis. That trade-off is wrong for this project: the user wants the RNA track aligned to *the donor's actual genotype*, including indels, not to a shared reference with SNPs masked out. The original TL;DR and Section 8 are replaced below; sections 1–7 are preserved as the original trade-off analysis but should be read with this re-decision in mind.

For this project (per-bp coverage tracks faithful to each donor's genotype, for a DNA LM that sees each donor's actual DNA), the pipeline is:

1. **Build a per-donor diploid FASTA with SNPs *and* indels physically inserted** from the donor's phased HipSci VCF. Use `g2gtools` (preferred — emits chain files and lifted GTFs) or `bcftools consensus -H 1 / -H 2` (simpler — no chain files). Output: `donorX_hap1.fa`, `donorX_hap2.fa`. No N-masking.

   > **Why both haplotypes physically materialized?** Because "the donor's genotype" is two distinct DNA sequences, and you want the RNA track aligned to *both* of them at their native coordinates. N-masking erases the het sites; a haploid consensus picks one allele and discards the other. A diploid reference is the only option that preserves both haplotypes losslessly.

   > **WHAT DOES PHASED MEAN?** The donor's VCF lists variants, but a VCF alone doesn't tell you which variants sit on the same physical chromosome. "Phasing" adds that information. The HipSci VCFs are statistically phased with SHAPEIT, which infers it from population patterns. Phasing matters here because you're building two haplotype FASTAs and need to know which variant goes on which.

2. **Align reads in diploid mode** to `donorX_hap1.fa + donorX_hap2.fa`. Two reasonable choices:
   - **`bowtie + EMASE`** (Churchill-lab pattern, Skelly 2020): align with bowtie, then run EMASE EM to probabilistically apportion reads that multi-map between haplotypes. This is the principled way to handle reads with no SNP/indel overlap (the majority).
   - **STAR diploid index** (concatenated `hap1 || hap2` contigs): faster and splice-aware, but multi-mapper handling is heuristic. Acceptable if you accept "primary alignment wins, ties broken by score."

3. **Generate per-haplotype BigWigs at base resolution** in each haplotype's *native coordinate space* — `donorX_hap1.bw`, `donorX_hap2.bw`. These are not in GRCh37/38 coordinates; they are in donor-X-hap1 and donor-X-hap2 coordinates. That is the point.

4. **No N-masking, no SNPsplit, no WASP.** These are the right tools when you want a single shared reference with debiased SNP coverage; they are the wrong tools when you want the track on the donor's actual sequence. SNPsplit specifically cannot do indel-aware splitting; WASP is a debiaser for a shared reference.

### What about cross-donor coordinate alignment?

It's now a **model-level problem**, not an alignment-level problem. The MSA-style architecture in `CLAUDE.md` fits this naturally: an MSA is itself a cross-donor coordinate alignment, with gaps representing indels. Two natural ways to feed the model:

- **(a) Per-donor coords + explicit alignment in the model.** Build an MSA over donor haplotype FASTAs (or use g2gtools' chain files) and let attention work across donors through the alignment. Indels become gap tokens. Cleanest match to the MSA idea.
- **(b) Per-donor coords end-to-end, no cross-donor coord mapping.** Each donor is its own training example, no cross-donor attention. Loses the variation-over-genotypes lever in the architecture but is the simplest baseline.

The N-masked-on-GRCh38 pipeline is what you'd build if you wanted option (c) "stay in GRCh38 coords forever," which is the wrong choice for this project. Sections 1–5 below document why that pipeline is the consensus in the *ASE* literature — and why ASE is a different problem from yours.

> **WHAT'S ASE:** Allele-Specific Expression — for each het site, the *ratio* of reads from one allele vs the other. ASE tools optimize for unbiased ratios at known het sites. Your goal is the *absolute coverage at every base* of the donor's actual sequence. Different optimization target, different right answer. The ASE-literature consensus (N-masking + WASP) optimizes the wrong thing for this project.

## 1. Standard reference + WASP filtering (van de Geijn et al. 2015)

**(a) What it does.** Align reads to plain GRCh38. At every heterozygous SNP a read covers, computationally flip the allele (REF↔ALT) and remap. If the modified read fails to map, maps to a different locus, or maps to multiple loci, the original read is reference-biased and is discarded.

**(b) Tools / papers.**
- Original: van de Geijn, McVicker, Gilad, Pritchard. *Nature Methods* 2015 — **WASP**: allele-specific software for robust molecular QTL discovery ([PMC4626402](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4626402/)).
- Integrated into STAR (`--waspOutputMode SAMtag --varVCFfile`) — Kaminow et al., bioRxiv 2024, *STAR+WASP reduces reference bias* ([PMC10871176](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10871176/)). 6.5–10× faster than standalone WASP, peak RAM increase <0.2%.
- Downstream ASE counting: GATK `ASEReadCounter`, `phASER` ([Castel et al. *Nat Commun* 2016, PMC5025529](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5025529/)), `SCReadCounts` for single cell.
- This is exactly what **Cuomo et al. 2020** used (see §7 below).

**(c) When recommended.** This is the default consensus best practice for ASE in bulk and Smart-seq2 single-cell RNA-seq. WASP outperformed N-masked and personalized-genome alignment in the original benchmark: "reads mapped to the N-masked and personalized genomes were substantially biased … reads mapped using WASP were almost perfectly balanced."

**(d) Limitations.**
- Discards reads rather than rescuing them, so coverage at heterozygous positions is reduced (typically 4–10% of reads at het sites are dropped; STAR+WASP paper reports residual bias of 0–5% after filtering vs 4–10% before).
- Operates on a single linear reference (GRCh38), so reads with private donor variants the VCF doesn't know about are still subject to bias.
- WASP corrects bias at *known* het sites; it does nothing for indels, structural variants, or unannotated variation.

## 2. Diploid reference (two haplotype FASTAs aligned to in parallel)

**(a) What it does.** Build two FASTA files (`donor_hap1.fa`, `donor_hap2.fa`) from the donor's phased VCF, either concatenated into one index or aligned to separately. Each read can map to either haplotype; reads that prefer one haplotype get assigned to it.

**(b) Tools / papers.**
- **AlleleSeq** (Rozowsky, Abyzov, Gerstein et al., *Mol Syst Biol* 2011, PMID 21811232) — the canonical implementation: builds maternal + paternal haploid FASTAs from phased variants, aligns with Bowtie to each separately, then merges.
- **g2gtools** (Churchill lab, JAX) — creates personal diploid genomes and provides lift-over back to reference coordinates that *does not discard reads landing in indel regions* ([repo](https://github.com/churchill-lab/g2gtools)). Designed for the Collaborative Cross / Diversity Outbred mouse populations.
- **EMASE** (paired with g2gtools) — takes BAMs aligned to a diploid transcriptome and runs EM to apportion multi-mapped reads between alleles ([emase docs](https://emase.readthedocs.io/)).
- **Seqnature** — older tool, individualized diploid genome construction (Munger et al., *Genetics* 2014, PMC4174954).
- **AltHapAlignR** (PMC6041798) — for HLA / MHC alternative haplotype handling.

**(c) When recommended.** Population genetics studies where individuals are highly heterozygous or carry indels/SVs relative to reference (mouse F1 hybrids, multiparent crosses, HLA region). g2gtools is essentially built for this. Also recommended when indels matter and N-masking is therefore insufficient.

**(d) Limitations / complications.**
- **Multi-mapping explosion.** Every read with no overlapping variant maps equally well to both haplotypes, producing duplicate primary alignments. You must decide: random choice (loses info), assign-to-best (introduces bias from random tie-breaking), or EM (g2gtools/EMASE approach — slow and not designed for short single-cell reads).
- **Read pair distance / paired-end issues.** With concatenated dual-FASTA, mate pairs can split across haplotypes and STAR may flag them as discordant or chimeric. With separate alignments, you have two BAMs and have to reconcile.
- **Coordinate system.** Output is in haplotype coordinates, not GRCh38. For a cross-donor BigWig you must lift back to a common reference (g2gtools supports this, but introduces its own ambiguity at indels).
- **Spliced aligners are not designed for this.** STAR is built for a single linear reference + a junction database. Forcing it onto a diploid index multiplies the seeding work and the junction database does not transfer cleanly between haplotypes.

For these reasons, dual-FASTA is rarely used as the primary alignment for short bulk/single-cell RNA-seq in human studies; it's the standard for mouse F1 ASE work where SNP density and indel rates make N-masking less effective.

## 3. N-masked reference (replace het sites with N or IUPAC)

**(a) What it does.** Build one consensus per-donor FASTA where heterozygous positions are replaced with `N`. Homozygous-alt positions can be substituted with the alt base (so the consensus is closer to the donor than GRCh38). Reads align to this single linear reference. Because both ref and alt allele match `N` equally, the aligner shows no preference.

**(b) Tools / papers.**
- **SNPsplit** (Krueger & Andrews, *F1000Research* 2016, [PMC4934512](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4934512/)) — primary tool; includes a `SNPsplit_genome_preparation` script that builds N-masked references from a VCF. Built for mouse F1 hybrids but works on any species with a VCF.
- `bedtools maskfasta` or `bcftools consensus --haplotype I` can produce an N-masked FASTA manually.
- **STAR handles `N` bases natively** (it's part of the standard 5-letter alphabet ACGTN). Aligns through N with no mismatch penalty for an A/C/G/T read base against a reference N. (Note: STAR does *not* support IUPAC ambiguity codes like R/Y/W — only N.)
- HISAT2, Bowtie2, BWA, Bismark, TopHat all also support N-masked references.

**(c) When recommended.** Best single-step bias mitigation when you don't need per-allele assignment — i.e., you want a clean "expression-level" coverage signal regardless of which allele a read came from. This matches your use case for the DNA LM (you want a track that says "transcription level at this base"). Also the workhorse for ChIP-seq / ATAC-seq ASE because of its simplicity.

**(d) Limitations.**
- Loses any reference-allele *match bonus* the aligner would give — the aligner can't tell the difference between a het site and a sequencing error at that base. This is the goal, but it does very slightly reduce specificity in repetitive regions.
- Does not handle indels — these still create mismatch/soft-clip biases. You can extend by N-masking indel positions and a window around them, but that's lossy.
- The Cuomo et al. 2020 paper benchmark and the WASP benchmark both showed N-masking alone leaves residual reference bias (because reads with multiple het sites can still partially match the original reference better than the flipped configuration). **N-masking + WASP filtering on top is strictly better than either alone** — and that combined pipeline is what is recommended here.
- **SNPsplit specifically requires soft-clipping disabled** (`--outSAMtype BAM SortedByCoordinate --alignEndsType EndToEnd` in STAR) because it cannot interpret soft-clipped reads. If you only want N-masked alignment without SNPsplit's allele assignment, this restriction does not apply.

## 4. Haploid consensus reference (pick one allele per het site)

**(a) What it does.** Generate a single FASTA per donor by picking one allele at each het site — either randomly (`bcftools consensus --haplotype R`), the major-population allele, or one phased haplotype. The result is a haploid pseudo-personalized reference.

**(b) Tools / papers.**
- `bcftools consensus -H 1` or `-H 2` for phased haplotype-1 / haplotype-2.
- Original "personalized genome" approach used in early AlleleSeq work.

**(c) When recommended.** Almost never for ASE work, because it bakes in a 50/50 reference bias *toward the chosen allele* at every het site. Sometimes used for variant calling pipelines on highly diverged individuals where the goal is to minimize mapping cost.

**(d) Limitations.**
- **This is the worst option for ASE / unbiased coverage** because it converts mapping bias from "toward GRCh38" to "toward the chosen donor allele." The bias is just as strong, just in a different direction.
- van de Geijn / WASP benchmark explicitly showed this approach is substantially biased relative to WASP.
- Only acceptable if the goal is downstream variant calling, not expression quantification.

## 5. Specialized pseudo-diploid / allele-splitting tools

**(a) What they do.** These tools sit on top of one of the alignment strategies above (usually N-masked) and post-hoc partition reads into haplotype-resolved BAMs using the phased genotype information.

**(b) Tools.**
- **SNPsplit** — N-masked alignment, then tag each read as `G1` (ref-matching), `G2` (alt-matching), `UA` (unassigned, no SNP overlap), or `CF` (conflicting, has SNPs matching both). Outputs two BAMs.
- **g2gtools + EMASE** — diploid genome alignment with EM probabilistic apportionment to alleles, lifted back to reference coordinates. More principled for indels.
- **phASER** (Castel et al. *Nat Commun* 2016) — works on standard reference alignment; uses RNA-seq reads spanning multiple variants to phase haplotypes and quantify haplotypic expression. Good for rare-variant phasing across long distances.
- **SCReadCounts** — single-cell ASE counting per cell.
- **Ornaments** (Sullivan et al., 2024, [PMC11339617](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11339617/)) — extension of kallisto that does WASP-style bias correction faster than WASP itself.
- **iMapSplice** (PMC6086400) — splice-aware personalized RNA-seq alignment.

**(c) When recommended.** When you specifically need *per-haplotype* tracks rather than a summed diploid track. For BigWig coverage at base-pair resolution, this is the only way to get separate hap1/hap2 tracks.

**(d) Limitations.** All inherit the SNP-density problem: a read that doesn't overlap any heterozygous SNP cannot be assigned to either haplotype. For Smart-seq2 with ~200 bp reads and typical human SNP density (~1 het / 1 kb), the unassignable fraction is usually 60–80% of reads. Those reads still contribute to a *diploid* signal but cannot be split.

## 6. Considerations specific to BigWig base-pair coverage tracks

The asymmetry between **ASE quantification** (counting ref vs alt reads at known het sites) and **coverage tracks** (every base of the genome should have a defensible number) matters here.

- **For a diploid summed track:** N-masked reference + STAR with `--waspOutputMode SAMtag --varVCFfile` + filter to `vW:i:1` reads + `deeptools bamCoverage --binSize 1` is the cleanest path. The N-masking removes seed-extend bias at het sites; the WASP filter catches residual bias from multi-SNP reads; the coverage at every base is comparable across donors because the output is in shared GRCh38 coordinates.
- **For per-haplotype tracks:** N-masked alignment + SNPsplit → two haplotype BAMs → two BigWigs. Unassigned reads (`UA`) can either be (a) dropped from both tracks (lose 60–80% of coverage), (b) added to both at half weight (cleaner but assumes equal allelic expression), or (c) added to a third "ambiguous" track. For an MSA-style DNA LM input, option (b) or (c) is usually preferable to (a).
- **Indels are a special problem for coverage tracks.** If you align to N-masked GRCh38, donor-specific insertions are missing from the reference and reads containing them get soft-clipped or split. Donor-specific deletions create false coverage spikes at flanking positions. For full base-pair fidelity you need either (i) personalized FASTAs with the donor's indels inserted, then lift back to GRCh38 with `g2gtools`, or (ii) graph-based alignment (vg, HISAT2 graph). The impute-first / personalized-pangenome literature ([Sirén et al. 2024, PMC10760139](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10760139/); [Mun et al. 2024, PMC10705441](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10705441/)) shows this matters for DNA-seq variant calling but explicitly notes "more work is required to establish its applicability to RNA-seq."
- **Normalization.** For a DNA-LM training input you probably want per-base coverage normalized to library depth (RPGC / CPM in `deeptools bamCoverage`) so cells with different sequencing depths are comparable.
- **No literature specifically benchmarks alignment strategies for BigWig coverage tracks from personalized references.** The existing benchmarks all evaluate ASE accuracy (binary REF/ALT counts at het sites). The coverage-track use case is novel; we are extrapolating the ASE recommendations.

## 7. What Cuomo et al. (2020) actually did

From the PMC methods (PMC7010688):

- **Reference:** "Trimmed reads were mapped to the human reference genome build 37 using STAR (version: 020201)" — i.e. plain GRCh37, *not* personalized.
- **Aligner mode:** "in two-pass alignment mode, using the default settings proposed by the ENCODE consortium."
- **Quantification:** "Gene-level expression quantification was performed using Salmon (version: 0.8.2), using the '--seqBias', '--gcBias' and 'VBOpt' options using ENSEMBL transcripts (built 75)."
- **Duplicates:** "Duplicated reads were removed from the STAR alignments using Picard Tools."
- **ASE counting:** "ASE counts were obtained using GATK tools v3.7 in ASEReadCounter mode, with the settings '-minDepth 1 -minMappingQuality 10 -minBaseQuality 2 -rf DuplicateRead'."
- **Genotypes:** "Genotypes were then phased using SHAPEIT v2.r790 and imputed, per sample, using IMPUTE2 v2.3.1."

Key takeaway: **Cuomo et al. used the simplest approach — plain GRCh37 + GATK ASEReadCounter with mapping-quality filtering, no WASP, no N-masking, no personalized genome.** They did not correct for reference mapping bias beyond the MQ ≥10 filter. The ASE counts they published on Zenodo (`ase_aggregated_by_donor_open_access_lines.tar.gz`) inherit this bias.

This is consistent with HipSci's standard RNA-seq pipeline (http://www.hipsci.org/assay/rnaseq/) which also maps to GRCh37 + STAR with standard ENCODE settings — they did not run a personalized pipeline. The Cuomo paper was published in 2020; STAR+WASP integration matured later (2024). For a contemporary re-analysis we should do better than they did.

## 8. Recommendation for this project (REVISED 2026-06-22)

Build a per-donor pipeline that materializes both haplotypes (SNPs + indels) and aligns in diploid mode. Concretely:

**Step 1 — Per-donor diploid FASTA construction (one-time per donor)**
```bash
# Inputs: HipSci phased VCF for donor X, hs37d5 FASTA (HipSci's reference)
# Outputs: donorX_hap1.fa, donorX_hap2.fa
#          donorX_hap1.chain, donorX_hap2.chain     (g2gtools only)
#          donorX_hap1.gtf, donorX_hap2.gtf         (g2gtools only)

# ---- Option A: g2gtools (preferred — chain files + lifted GTFs) ----
# Pattern from Skelly 2020 / Churchill-lab DO mouse pipeline.
# Exact CLI varies by g2gtools version; this is the conceptual flow:
g2gtools vcf2vci -i hipsci.phased.vcf.gz -s donorX -p 1 -o donorX_hap1.vci
g2gtools patch     -i hs37d5.fa -c donorX_hap1.vci.gz -o donorX_hap1_patched.fa
g2gtools transform -i donorX_hap1_patched.fa -c donorX_hap1.vci.gz -o donorX_hap1.fa
g2gtools convert   -i gencode.v19.annotation.gtf -c donorX_hap1.vci.gz -o donorX_hap1.gtf
# repeat with -p 2 for hap2

# ---- Option B: bcftools consensus (simpler — no chain files, no lifted GTF) ----
bcftools consensus -H 1 -f hs37d5.fa -s donorX hipsci.phased.vcf.gz > donorX_hap1.fa
bcftools consensus -H 2 -f hs37d5.fa -s donorX hipsci.phased.vcf.gz > donorX_hap2.fa
# You still need lifted GTFs and chain files to relate hap coords back to GRCh37 —
# use CrossMap or g2gtools post-hoc, or just train the model in per-donor coords.
```

**Step 2 — Build a per-donor diploid index**
```bash
# Concatenate the two haplotype FASTAs with disambiguated contig names
sed 's/^>/>hap1_/' donorX_hap1.fa  > donorX_diploid.fa
sed 's/^>/>hap2_/' donorX_hap2.fa >> donorX_diploid.fa

# Build a STAR index from the diploid FASTA (no GTF baked in — pass at alignment time)
STAR --runMode genomeGenerate \
  --genomeDir star_idx_donorX \
  --genomeFastaFiles donorX_diploid.fa
```

**Step 3 — Per-cell diploid alignment**
```bash
# STAR with multi-mapping allowed between haplotypes
STAR --runMode alignReads \
  --genomeDir star_idx_donorX \
  --readFilesIn cell.R1.fq.gz cell.R2.fq.gz --readFilesCommand zcat \
  --sjdbGTFfile donorX_diploid.gtf \
  --twopassMode Basic \
  --outSAMtype BAM SortedByCoordinate \
  --outFilterMultimapNmax 2 \
  --outSAMmultNmax 2 \
  --outSAMattributes NH HI nM AS NM MD

# (Alternative — Churchill-lab pattern: bowtie + EMASE EM apportionment.
#  More principled for ambiguous reads but loses splice-awareness.)
```

**Step 4 — Per-haplotype coverage tracks**
```bash
# Split BAM into hap1 / hap2 by contig prefix
samtools view -bh cell.bam $(samtools view -H cell.bam | awk '/^@SQ/ && /hap1_/ {print $2}' | sed 's/SN://') > cell.hap1.bam
samtools view -bh cell.bam $(samtools view -H cell.bam | awk '/^@SQ/ && /hap2_/ {print $2}' | sed 's/SN://') > cell.hap2.bam

# BigWigs in *native haplotype coordinates*
bamCoverage -b cell.hap1.bam -o cell.hap1.bw --binSize 1 --normalizeUsing CPM
bamCoverage -b cell.hap2.bam -o cell.hap2.bw --binSize 1 --normalizeUsing CPM
```

The CLI for g2gtools above is illustrative — verify exact flag names against the current `churchill-lab/g2gtools` README before running. The pipeline shape is what matters: VCF → per-haplotype FASTA + GTF + chain → diploid index → diploid alignment → per-haplotype coverage.

**Why this is the right shape for this project:**
- Indels are physically present in each haplotype's sequence. Reads spanning a donor-specific insertion or deletion map without soft-clipping or false coverage spikes.
- Het SNPs are *not* erased — both alleles are present in the index. The aligner is free to prefer the correct haplotype.
- Output BigWigs are in *donor-haplotype coordinates*, faithful to the actual DNA the RNA was transcribed from. This is what the DNA LM should be conditioned on.
- Multi-mapping reads (no SNP/indel overlap) are handled by either EMASE (principled EM) or STAR's primary-alignment-wins heuristic. Either is defensible; EMASE is the gold standard.

**Caveats / open questions:**
1. **Diploid alignment is harder than N-masked single-reference alignment.** Multi-mapping reads at conserved positions are real and have to be handled — that's what EMASE is for. Without EMASE (e.g., STAR's heuristic primary-pick), expect ~50% of multi-mapped reads to be assigned arbitrarily between haplotypes at conserved sites. For *coverage*-summed-across-haplotypes this is fine; for per-haplotype quantitation it's lossy.
2. **Cross-donor coordinate alignment is now a downstream problem.** Donor A's hap1 position 12345 is not the same locus as donor B's hap1 position 12345 (because of indels upstream). You need (a) an MSA over donor FASTAs to align them in the model, (b) g2gtools chain files to lift everything to GRCh37 (lossy at indel regions), or (c) accept per-donor-coord training examples with no cross-donor attention.
3. **Compute cost.** Per-donor diploid STAR index = ~60 GB × 125 donors = ~7.5 TB. Worth provisioning against, or building indices lazily per training shard. Bowtie indices (Skelly/Churchill route) are much smaller (~3 GB each).
4. **Smart-seq2 read length and splice handling.** Cuomo data is HiSeq 2000 PE 75-100 bp. STAR's splice-awareness is valuable for full-length sc data; the EMASE/bowtie route loses this. If you go bowtie+EMASE you need to handle splicing separately (Skelly 2020 uses pooled founder *transcriptomes* rather than genomes, which sidesteps splicing entirely but doesn't give per-bp genome coverage).
5. **No published benchmark for the BigWig-on-donor-coords use case.** ASE-literature benchmarks evaluate ref/alt count accuracy at het sites — a different problem. A small sanity-check experiment (e.g., compare hap1 BigWig from g2gtools+STAR vs g2gtools+bowtie+EMASE on a few cells) is worth doing before committing 125 donors.
